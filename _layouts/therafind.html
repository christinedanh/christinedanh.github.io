Secondary Research
To gain a better understanding of the problem, I started by exploring existing data relating to the challenges of finding therapy. Researching the space confirmed that accessing mental health services is a challenge.

Secondary research also revealed why accessing mental health services can be a challenge:
  1. Some people have no insurance or limited coverage
  2. There is a disconnect between primary care system and behavioral health system
  3. People feel like they have limited options (because they need to stay in-network)
  4. Difficult finding available in-network providers Expensive when out-of-network

Next, I conducted five user interviews to find answers to my unanswered questions. I recruited individuals who are currently seeking therapy or have sought out therapy in the past. Now that I know who to design for, I had to define the key problems to solve. Our core question became:

Low Fidelity Mock Up
Having a clear flow mapped out, I began sketching what the screens might look like.
Guerilla testing the lo-fi helped me get a sense of what was working and what was not.
  1. Users had differing opinions on the flow.
    1. Users had differing opinions on the flow. Originally, users were asked to create an account at the time of booking an appointment, similarly to the checkout experience for online shopping.
    2. Some users were confused about why sign up was so late in the process. Others liked the ability to explore before committing to creating an account.
    3. When it came to wireframing, I opted to allow users to decide their flow. They would be able to either sign up/log in from the get-go or explore first.
  2. Icons without labels were confusing to some users.
    1. Users commented about being unsure what would happen upon clicking some icons and buttons.
    2. In the wireframe, I labeled every icon and button to reduce uncertainty.
  3. The homepage was confusing.
    1. Some users were not sure how to proceed from the homepage.
    2. The unlabeled icons played a role in that.

Settling On A Style
After having a better picture of what the app would look like, I began to think about the visual design. I created a moodboard for inspiration and came up with the style guide. Here's a sneak peek into my moodboard and style guide:
Understanding that mental health can be a sensitive topic, I had a goal of choosing styles that would help users feel at ease. Testing The Prototype
Taking the style guide I created, I checked it for accessibility and began to apply it to a high fidelity mockup. I then converted it into a prototype and conducted two rounds of usability testing. No major usability issues were found from user testing, but minor issues were identified.
Testing: Round 1
Issue #1
Not all users are familiar with technical terms used in therapy. Therapist credentials, therapist titles, and therapy specialties were not fully understood by all users. The current solution of having a separate section designated to learning about therapy proved to be inefficient. It required users to navigate between screens to understand what they were reading. Most users were also not aware of that feature.
Solution
Add information icons next to each technical term. When clicked on, a pop up would appear with an explanation of the term.
Issue #2
There was no way for users to contact the therapist if they had inquiries.
Solution
Add a phone number and a website link for users to learn more or reach out to the provider. Also added a "contact" button that allows users to email the provider directly from the app.
Issue #3
Some users were unsure if the therapists they were looking at were accepting new patients. It wasn't until the availability page that users discovered that they were taking appointments.
Solution
Change the search results to say "(number of) available therapists" rather than "(number of) therapists found." Another solution would be to add an option to filter for therapists who are accepting new patients.
Issue #4
The price range for each therapist is not shown on the search results page. Users don't know the cost until they reach the therapist profile page.
Solution
Add a price range to each therapist on the search results page.
Testing: Round 2
Issue #1
Some users expressed confusion about the purpose of the "sign in later" button.
Solution
Reword "sign in later" to something that not only labels the function of the button, but also the purpose. For example, "continue as guest" or "browse before creating an account."
Issue #2
The current filter options do not cover everything that users might want to filter for.
Solution
In the next iteration, I would add filters, additional filters such as language spoken, ethnicity, credentials, and multiple insurances.
Issue #3
Some users were unsure if the therapists they were looking at were accepting new patients. It wasn't until the availability page that users discovered that they were taking appointments.
Solution
In the next iteration, I changed the search results to say "(number of) available therapists" rather than "(number of) therapists found. Another solution would be to add an option to the filtering page to filter for therapists who are accepting new patients.
Testing: Conclusions
Though the minor issues, all 10 of 10 users easily completed the tasks of searching for a compatible therapist and booking an appointment. Missteps were not observed.
Many noted that TheraFind is more encompassing than the alternatives currently available. It checks off all the boxes. Users noted that there are apps and websites out there that give users the ability to explore therapists, but only TheraFind gives them the confidence that the therapists being shown are covered by their insurance, helping them save on costs. Not only does it allow users to find therapists who suit their needs, they're able to book a session with the therapist in the same place. Users were grateful to be able to avoid phone calls and emails that often go unanswered.

What I learned
Being my first ever design project, I learned so much.
  1. My ideas aren't always going to work for everyone. And that's okay.
  2. Testing and talking to users throughout the process not only helped me uncover issues with my designs, but also kept me in touch with users and their needs. Speaking with users frequently helped me keep my focus on designing a solution for the target audience and not for myself.
  3. Finally, I learned that the job is never done. There are always more questions to ask and things to improve.
